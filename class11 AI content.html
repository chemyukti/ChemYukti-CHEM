🤔 What is Artificial Intelligence?

Definition Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the science and engineering of making machines capable of performing tasks that would normally require human intelligence — such as reasoning, learning from experience, understanding language, recognising patterns, and making decisions.

Think of it this way: when you ask Google Maps for the fastest route and it accounts for real-time traffic, that's AI at work. When Netflix recommends a show you end up loving, that's AI. When your phone unlocks by recognising your face — also AI.

Real-life analogy A baby learns to recognise a cat by seeing hundreds of cats. An AI image classifier does the same thing — it is shown thousands of labelled pictures of cats until it builds its own internal "understanding" of what makes something a cat. Same process, different substrate.

The core idea of AI has two parts: perception (sensing data from the world — images, text, speech) and cognition (making sense of that data to decide or predict something useful).

📅 Evolution of AI — A Brief History

1950 — Turing Test

Alan Turing proposes that a machine can be called intelligent if a human cannot distinguish its responses from a human's in conversation.

1956 — The Birth of AI

John McCarthy coins the term "Artificial Intelligence" at the Dartmouth Conference. Researchers were optimistic — many thought human-level AI was 20 years away.

1970s–80s — AI Winters

Progress stalled. Computing power was too weak and data was too scarce. Funding dried up — twice — leading to pessimism known as "AI Winters."

1997 — Deep Blue beats Kasparov

IBM's chess computer defeats the world chess champion, proving that machines could outperform humans in complex strategic games.

2012 — Deep Learning Revolution

AlexNet wins the ImageNet challenge with a massive lead using deep neural networks. GPUs + big data + better algorithms change everything.

2016 — AlphaGo

Google DeepMind's AI defeats the world Go champion — a game thought to be too complex for machines for decades.

2022–present — Generative AI Era

Large Language Models (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) and image generators bring AI to everyday users. AI becomes a daily tool, not just a research topic.

🧩 Types of AI

AI is classified by capability — how "smart" it is compared to a human:

🎯 Narrow AI (Weak AI)

  • Designed for one specific task
  • Cannot generalise to other tasks
  • All current real-world AI is Narrow AI
  • Examples: Siri, spam filters, face recognition, chess engines

🧠 General AI (Strong AI / AGI)

  • Can perform any intellectual task a human can
  • Can reason, learn, adapt across domains
  • Does not exist yet — theoretical goal
  • Example: A robot that can cook, write code, and counsel patients equally well

🚀 Super AI (Artificial Superintelligence)

Hypothetical AI that surpasses the best human intelligence in all domains. Purely theoretical. Subject of philosophical and ethical debate.

Exam tip When asked "what type is ChatGPT or Alexa?" — the answer is always Narrow AI. No current AI system qualifies as General or Super AI.

📖 Key AI Terminologies

Machine Learning (ML)A subset of AI where systems learn from data without being explicitly programmed for every scenario. Instead of writing rules, you feed examples and the system finds the rules itself.
Deep Learning (DL)A subset of ML that uses artificial neural networks with many layers (hence "deep"). It excels at tasks like image recognition and language understanding. Powers most modern AI breakthroughs.
Neural NetworkA computing system inspired by the structure of the human brain, consisting of interconnected nodes (neurons) that process information in layers.
Training DataThe labelled examples used to teach an AI model. More quality data = better model. Garbage in, garbage out.
AlgorithmA set of rules or instructions that a computer follows to solve a problem or make a decision. In AI, algorithms process data to produce outputs like predictions or classifications.

❓ Questions & Answers

Q1. Define Artificial Intelligence. How is it different from traditional programming? +
In traditional programming, a programmer writes explicit rules (if-then logic) for every situation. In AI, especially Machine Learning, the system learns the rules itself by studying examples. For instance, a traditional spam filter has hard-coded rules like "block emails containing 'lottery'." An AI spam filter learns from thousands of spam and non-spam emails and discovers complex patterns on its own — it can even catch new types of spam it has never seen before.
Q2. What is the Turing Test? Is it still relevant? +
The Turing Test (1950), proposed by Alan Turing, checks if a machine's conversational responses are indistinguishable from a human's to a human evaluator. If the evaluator cannot tell whether they are chatting with a machine or a person, the machine is said to pass the test. It remains conceptually influential but is now considered an incomplete measure of intelligence — a machine might game the test through clever responses without any real understanding.
Q3. Classify the following as Narrow AI, General AI, or Super AI: (a) Siri, (b) A hypothetical robot that can cure cancer and write poetry, (c) Chess engine. +
(a) Siri — Narrow AI (designed specifically for voice-based Q&A and phone tasks). (b) The hypothetical robot — Super AI (surpasses human-level intelligence across multiple domains). (c) Chess engine — Narrow AI (extremely good at chess, but useless at anything else).
Q4. List three real-life applications of AI for each domain: Computer Vision, NLP, and Machine Learning. +
Computer Vision: Face recognition on smartphones, tumor detection in medical scans, quality control in manufacturing (detecting defective products). NLP: Google Translate, Grammarly, virtual assistants like Alexa. Machine Learning: Netflix/Spotify recommendations, credit card fraud detection, weather prediction.
Q5. What is Deep Learning and how does it differ from Machine Learning? +
Machine Learning is a broad field where systems learn from data. Deep Learning is a specific approach within ML that uses multi-layered neural networks to automatically learn features from raw data (like pixels in images). Traditional ML requires a human expert to manually engineer features (e.g., "look at edges for face detection"). Deep Learning discovers these features automatically, making it extremely powerful for complex tasks like speech recognition and image classification, but it also requires far more data and computing power.

❓ Q&A

Q1. What is the difference between a list and a tuple in Python? +
Both are ordered sequences of elements. The key difference: a list is mutable (you can change, add, remove elements after creation). A tuple is immutable (cannot be changed once created). Use tuples for data that should not change — like GPS coordinates, or days of the week.
Q2. Why do we use NumPy arrays instead of Python lists for AI? +
Python lists store elements of any type and use more memory. NumPy arrays store elements of a single type (homogeneous) and use far less memory. Crucially, NumPy operations are vectorised — they run in C under the hood, making them 10–100× faster than equivalent loops. For AI with millions of data points, this speed is not optional — it's essential.
Q3. What does df.describe() return in Pandas? +
It returns a statistical summary of all numerical columns in the DataFrame: count (non-null values), mean, standard deviation, minimum, 25th percentile (Q1), median (50th percentile/Q2), 75th percentile (Q3), and maximum. It's the fastest way to get an overview of your data's distribution.

🎨 Design Thinking

Design ThinkingA human-centred problem-solving framework that prioritises deeply understanding users before proposing solutions. It moves through five phases: Empathise → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test. Unlike traditional engineering (solution first), Design Thinking starts with people.
Design Thinking — 5 Phases
1. Empathise
Understand users → 2. Define
Frame the problem → 3. Ideate
Brainstorm solutions
← 5. Test
Get feedback ← 4. Prototype
Build a model

Notice: the process is iterative, not linear. After testing, you may go back to re-empathise or re-define the problem.

🗺️ The 5W1H Method

Before jumping to a solution, decompose any problem using 6 questions:

QuestionExample (Problem: Students skip breakfast)
Who is affected?Class IX–XII students, especially those who travel long distances
What is the problem?Skipping breakfast leads to poor concentration in morning classes
When does it happen?On school days, especially during exam seasons
Where does it happen?Low-income neighbourhoods; students with long commutes
Why does it happen?Lack of time, awareness, or access to nutritious food
How can we solve it?AI-powered meal planning app + school canteen alerts

❓ Q&A

Q1. What is Design Thinking? How is it different from traditional problem-solving? +
Design Thinking is a human-centred, iterative process that starts with deeply understanding user needs (Empathise) before defining the problem or building solutions. Traditional problem-solving often starts with a pre-defined problem statement and goes linearly to a solution. Design Thinking acknowledges that the real problem often isn't what it first appears to be — and encourages revisiting earlier stages based on what is learned during testing.
Q2. What is an empathy map and why is it used? +
An empathy map is a visual tool with four quadrants — Says, Thinks, Does, Feels — that captures the inner and outer world of your target user. It's used to move the design team from assumptions ("we think users want X") to evidence-based insight ("users actually feel Y and do Z"). It prevents building solutions that look good in theory but fail in practice because they don't match real user behaviour.
Q3. Name the five stages of Design Thinking and describe the Ideate stage. +
The five stages are: Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test. The Ideate stage is where the team generates a wide range of creative solutions to the defined problem — without immediate judgement. Techniques include brainstorming, mind mapping, and the "How Might We" (HMW) method. The goal is quantity first, quality later — generate 20 ideas before narrowing to the best 2–3 for prototyping.

📈 Statistical Analysis — Python Code

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

marks = [72, 85, 90, 65, 88, 72, 95, 60, 78, 72]

# Central tendency
print("Mean:  ", np.mean(marks))         # 77.7
print("Median:", np.median(marks))        # 76.5
print("Mode:  ", stats.mode(marks).mode[0]) # 72

# Spread
print("Std Dev:", np.std(marks))         # 11.2
print("Variance:", np.var(marks))        # 125.4
print("Range:  ", max(marks)-min(marks)) # 35
Mean = sum of all values / count. Sensitive to outliers.
Median = middle value when sorted. Robust to outliers.
Mode = most frequent value. Useful for categorical data.
Standard Deviation = average distance of values from the mean. Small σ = data clustered tightly; large σ = data spread widely.

❓ Q&A

Q1. Differentiate between a histogram and a bar graph. +
A bar graph represents categorical data — each bar is a separate category (e.g., months or subjects), and the bars have gaps between them. A histogram represents the distribution of continuous numerical data — it groups data into bins (intervals), and the bars touch each other (no gaps) because the data is continuous. Use a histogram when you want to see how data is distributed (e.g., what percentage of students scored between 60–70).
Q2. What is data pre-processing and why is it necessary? +
Data pre-processing is the step of cleaning and transforming raw data before feeding it to an ML model. It includes: removing duplicates, handling missing values (filling with mean/median or dropping rows), normalising numerical features (scaling to the same range), encoding categorical variables into numbers (since ML models need numbers), and removing outliers. It's necessary because real-world data is messy — a model trained on uncleaned data gives unreliable results.
Q3. When would you use a scatter plot? +
A scatter plot is used to visualise the relationship (correlation) between two numerical variables — one on the x-axis, one on the y-axis. Each point represents one data sample. If the points form an upward trend → positive correlation (e.g., more study hours = higher score). Downward trend → negative correlation. No pattern → no relationship. It's the first tool for exploring whether a linear model might work on your data.

📏 Supervised Learning — Regression

Supervised LearningThe model is trained on labelled data — each input (features) has a corresponding correct output (label). The model learns the mapping from input to output. Think of a teacher-student relationship: the teacher (labelled data) guides the student (model).
Correlation vs CausationCorrelation measures the strength of the linear relationship between two variables (ranges from -1 to +1). Causation means one variable causes another — much harder to prove. "Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are correlated" — but ice cream doesn't cause drowning; summer heat causes both.

Pearson Correlation Coefficient:

Linear Regression — Finding the Line:

from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression

🏷️ Classification — k-Nearest Neighbour (kNN)

ClassificationA supervised learning task where the output is a category (class label) — e.g., spam/not spam, pass/fail, dog/cat. The model learns to assign new inputs to one of several classes.
k-Nearest Neighbour (kNN)A simple, intuitive classification algorithm: to classify a new point, find the k training points closest to it (neighbours) and take a majority vote of their labels. "You are who your neighbours are." k is a tunable hyperparameter — typically odd numbers like 3, 5, 7.
Intuition Imagine you move to a new city. To decide which restaurant is good, you ask your 5 nearest neighbours. If 4 say "The Dosa Place is great," that's where you go. kNN works the same way — the 5 nearest data points vote on the label. 
from sklearn.neighbors import KNeighborsClassifier

# New student: 4 hours study, 75% attendance

🔵 Unsupervised Learning — k-Means Clustering

Unsupervised LearningThe model learns from unlabelled data — finding hidden structure, groupings, or patterns without any guidance. There is no "correct answer" provided during training. The model discovers patterns on its own.
k-Means ClusteringAn algorithm that groups data into k clusters where each data point belongs to the cluster whose centroid (centre) is nearest. It iterates until cluster assignments stabilise. k must be chosen by the user.
Real-life analogy Imagine organising 100 unlabelled books on a shelf. k-Means is like placing k bookmarks randomly, then repeatedly reassigning each book to the nearest bookmark and moving each bookmark to the centre of its group — until the bookmarks stop moving. 
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Customer data: [annual_income, spending_score]
X = np.array([[15,39],[16,81],[17,6],[89,40],
              [88,41],[85,67],[27,77],[26,49]])

kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=3, random_state=42)
kmeans.fit(X)
labels = kmeans.labels_
print("Cluster labels:", labels)

# Visualise
plt.scatter(X[:,0], X[:,1], c=labels, cmap='Set1')
plt.scatter(kmeans.cluster_centers_[:,0], 
            kmeans.cluster_centers_[:,1], 
            marker='X', s=200, c='black')
plt.title("Customer Segments"); plt.show()

❓ Q&A

Q1. What is the difference between Supervised and Unsupervised Learning? Give examples. +
Supervised Learning uses labelled training data (each input has a correct answer). Examples: email spam detection (labelled spam/not spam), exam pass/fail prediction. Unsupervised Learning uses unlabelled data — the model finds its own structure. Examples: customer segmentation, news article topic grouping. The key test: does your training data have labels? Yes → supervised. No → unsupervised.
Q2. What is Linear Regression? Write the equation and explain each term. +
Linear Regression finds the best-fit straight line through data points. The equation is y = mx + b where: y = predicted output (dependent variable), x = input feature (independent variable), m = slope (how much y changes for 1 unit increase in x), b = intercept (value of y when x is 0). The "best" line is the one that minimises the sum of squared errors — the difference between predicted and actual y values.
Q3. In kNN, how does the choice of k affect the model? +
Small k (e.g., k=1): the model is very sensitive — it pays attention to individual noisy points, leading to overfitting (too complex, captures noise). Large k (e.g., k=100): the model considers too many neighbours and may oversimplify, leading to underfitting (misses real patterns). The best k is usually found by trying several values and measuring accuracy — often 3, 5, or 7. Using an odd k prevents ties in voting.

# ================================================
# 🍦 ICE CREAM CHATBOT — Rule-Based NLP
# CBSE AI Class XI — Unit 7 Practical

# ================================================

import random

# 1. KNOWLEDGE BASE — flavours, prices, sizes
MENU = {
    "vanilla": 40,
    "chocolate": 50,
    "strawberry": 45,
    "mango": 55,
    "butterscotch": 50
}
SIZES = {"small": 1, "medium": 1.5, "large": 2}
GREETINGS = ["hi", "hello", "hey", "namaste"]

# 2. TOKENISER — convert input to lowercase tokens
def tokenise(text):
    return text.lower().split()

# 3. INTENT DETECTOR — rule-based NLP
def detect_intent(tokens):
    if any(g in tokens for g in GREETINGS):
        return "greet"
    if "menu" in tokens or "flavours" in tokens:
        return "show_menu"
    if "order" in tokens or "want" in tokens or "give" in tokens:
        return "order"
    if "bye" in tokens or "exit" in tokens or "quit" in tokens:
        return "exit"
    return "unknown"

# 4. RESPONSE GENERATOR
def generate_response(intent, tokens, state):
    if intent == "greet":
        return "Welcome to Gelato Bot! 🍦 What flavour would you like? Type 'menu' to see options.", state
    
    if intent == "show_menu":
        menu_text = "Our menu:\n"
        for flavour, price in MENU.items():
            menu_text += f"  🍨 {flavour.capitalize()}: ₹{price}\n"
        menu_text += "Sizes: small, medium, large\nType: 'order [flavour] [size]'"
        return menu_text, state
    
    if intent == "order":
        flavour_found = None
        size_found = "medium"  # default
        for token in tokens:
            if token in MENU: flavour_found = token
            if token in SIZES: size_found = token
        if flavour_found:
            price = int(MENU[flavour_found] * SIZES[size_found])
            state["order"] = {"flavour": flavour_found, "size": size_found, "price": price}
            return f"Order confirmed! 🎉\n  {size_found.capitalize()} {flavour_found.capitalize()} → ₹{price}\nThank you! Enjoy! 😊", state
        else:
            return "Which flavour? Try: vanilla, chocolate, strawberry, mango, butterscotch", state
    
    if intent == "exit":
        return "Thank you for visiting! See you next time! 👋", {"active": False}
    
    return "I didn't understand that. Try: 'menu', 'order chocolate large', or 'bye'", state

# 5. MAIN CHATBOT LOOP
def run_chatbot():
    state = {"active": True, "order": None}
    print("🍦 Gelato Bot started! (type 'bye' to exit)\n")
    
    while state["active"]:
        user_input = input("You: ")
        tokens = tokenise(user_input)
        intent = detect_intent(tokens)
        response, state = generate_response(intent, tokens, state)
        print(f"Bot: {response}\n")

run_chatbot()
How Theory Maps to Code tokenise() = NLP Phase 1 (Tokenisation). detect_intent() = Classification (rule-based). Keywords in tokens = bag-of-words model. The if-elif chain = the classification algorithm. state dictionary = conversation memory / context. This is exactly how early commercial chatbots (early 2000s) worked before deep learning.

❓ Q&A

Q1. What is tokenisation? Why is it the first step in NLP? +
Tokenisation is the process of splitting a text string into individual units called tokens — usually words or subwords. Example: "AI is fun!" → ["AI", "is", "fun", "!"]. It is the first step because computers process discrete units, not continuous streams of characters. Before any analysis (grammar parsing, sentiment detection, translation) can happen, the text must be broken into manageable units.
Q2. Differentiate between a rule-based chatbot and an AI chatbot. +
Rule-based chatbot: Follows hand-written if-then rules and keyword matching. Cannot handle anything outside its ruleset. Fast, predictable, transparent. Good for narrow tasks (booking, FAQ). AI chatbot (ML-based): Trained on large datasets of conversations. Can generalise to new inputs, handle complex questions, maintain context. Powers systems like ChatGPT. Can sometimes produce incorrect or surprising outputs. The CBSE chatbot project is rule-based — it is simpler, fully explainable, and appropriate for introduction to NLP.
Q3. What is stemming? Give two examples. +
Stemming is a process that reduces a word to its root (stem) by stripping suffixes — without necessarily producing a valid dictionary word. It is used to normalise words so that "run", "running", "runner" are all treated as the same concept. Examples: "studying" → "studi" (Porter Stemmer), "happiness" → "happi". Lemmatisation is a more sophisticated alternative that produces valid base forms: "better" → "good", "running" → "run".

⚖️ Ethics in Artificial Intelligence

AI EthicsA field of applied ethics that addresses the moral implications of designing, building, deploying, and using AI systems. It asks: just because we CAN build this, SHOULD we? And if we do, how do we ensure it is fair, safe, and beneficial?

As AI becomes embedded in decisions that affect people's lives — who gets a loan, who gets hired, who a self-driving car prioritises — the ethical dimensions become urgent.

🏛️ The Five Pillars of AI Ethics

🔍

Transparency

AI systems should be understandable — users should know when they are interacting with AI and how decisions are made.

⚖️

Fairness

AI should treat all groups equitably. It should not discriminate based on race, gender, religion, or socioeconomic status.

🛡️

Accountability

There must always be a human or organisation responsible when AI causes harm. "The AI decided" is not an acceptable excuse.

🔒

Privacy

AI systems that collect personal data must protect it. Data should be used only with informed consent, and users must have control.

🌍

Beneficence (Do Good / Avoid Harm)

AI must benefit humanity and avoid causing harm — intended or unintended. Safety, reliability, and minimising risk are core design requirements.

🎯 Bias in AI

AI BiasSystematic and unfair discrimination in AI decisions, usually caused by biases present in the training data or introduced by the design choices of the development team. Bias in → Bias out.

Sources of Bias:

SourceExample
Historical data biasA hiring algorithm trained on past hires (mostly men) learns to prefer male candidates
Selection biasA skin disease detection model trained only on light-skinned patients performs poorly on darker skin tones
Confirmation biasDevelopers design systems that confirm their existing assumptions
Measurement biasProxy variables (like zip code) correlate with race, baking in discrimination
Reporting biasCertain events are over/under-reported in data (e.g., crime data biased toward policed areas)
Real Case Study: COMPAS COMPAS was an AI tool used by US courts to predict the likelihood of a defendant re-offending and inform sentencing. A 2016 investigation found it was twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as high-risk compared to white defendants. This is a real-world case of algorithmic bias causing direct harm to people's lives.

❓ Q&A

Q1. What are the five pillars of AI Ethics? Explain any two. +
The five pillars are: Transparency, Fairness, Accountability, Privacy, and Beneficence.
Transparency: AI systems should be explainable — users should know when AI is involved in a decision and how that decision was made. A loan rejection with no explanation violates transparency. Fairness: The system should not discriminate based on race, gender, religion, or other protected attributes. A facial recognition system that works well for white faces but poorly for dark-skinned faces is unfair — even if unintentionally so.
Q2. Explain bias in AI with a real-world example. How can it be reduced? +
AI bias occurs when a model produces systematically unfair or inaccurate results for certain groups — usually because the training data reflects historical inequalities. Example: Amazon scrapped an AI hiring tool in 2018 that penalised resumes containing the word "women's" (e.g., "women's chess club") because it was trained on a decade of resumes submitted mostly by men. Reduction strategies: collect diverse, representative training data; regularly audit model outputs across demographic groups; include diverse teams in development; apply fairness constraints; maintain human oversight for final decisions.
Q3. Why is "The AI decided" not an acceptable justification for a harmful outcome? +
This question relates to the principle of Accountability. AI systems are created and deployed by humans — they embody the choices, assumptions, and data of their creators. When an AI makes a harmful decision, the developers, the organisation that deployed it, and sometimes the regulators who permitted it all bear responsibility. Allowing "the algorithm decided" as a defence removes human accountability from consequential decisions. The EU AI Act and most ethical frameworks explicitly require that humans remain accountable for AI decisions, especially in high-stakes domains like healthcare, criminal justice, and finance.
